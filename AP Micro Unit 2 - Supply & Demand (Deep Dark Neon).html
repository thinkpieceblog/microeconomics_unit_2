① Supply & Demand — Market Equilibrium

The market reaches equilibrium where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied (Qe, Pe). The demand curve slopes downward (law of demand) and the supply curve slopes upward (law of supply).

Key Points

  • Equilibrium Price (Pe) — market clears, no surplus or shortage
  • Law of Demand — as price rises, Qd falls (inverse relationship)
  • Law of Supply — as price rises, Qs rises (direct relationship)
  • Above Pe → surplus (Qs > Qd) → price falls back to equilibrium
  • Below Pe → shortage (Qd > Qs) → price rises back to equilibrium

② Supply & Demand — Curve Shifts

When a non-price determinant changes, the entire curve shifts. Demand shifts change both P and Q in the same direction. Supply shifts change P and Q in opposite directions.

Key Points

  • Demand shifters — income, tastes, prices of related goods, expectations, # of buyers
  • Supply shifters — input costs, technology, # of sellers, expectations, government policy
  • D↑ → P↑ Q↑  |  D↓ → P↓ Q↓
  • S↑ → P↓ Q↑  |  S↓ → P↑ Q↓
  • Double shifts: one axis may be indeterminate

③ Consumer & Producer Surplus

Consumer Surplus (CS) is the area above price and below the demand curve. Producer Surplus (PS) is the area below price and above the supply curve. Together they form Total Economic Surplus.

Key Points

  • CS Consumer Surplus — willingness to pay minus actual price paid
  • PS Producer Surplus — price received minus marginal cost of production
  • Total Surplus = CS + PS — maximized at free-market equilibrium
  • Any deviation from equilibrium reduces total surplus → creates deadweight loss
  • Competitive market is allocatively efficient (P = MC at equilibrium)

④ Price Floor (Binding)

A price floor is a government-set minimum price above equilibrium. It causes a surplus (Qs > Qd) and creates deadweight loss. Example: minimum wage.

Key Points

  • Must be set above Pe to be binding (effective)
  • Results in a surplus Qs > Qd — quantity exchanged = Qd
  • CS decreases, PS may increase, DWL created Deadweight Loss
  • Examples: minimum wage, agricultural price supports
  • Quantity actually traded = the lesser of Qs or Qd = Qd

⑤ Price Ceiling (Binding)

A price ceiling is a government-set maximum price below equilibrium. It causes a shortage (Qd > Qs) and creates deadweight loss. Example: rent control.

Key Points

  • Must be set below Pe to be binding (effective)
  • Results in a shortage Qd > Qs — quantity exchanged = Qs
  • CS may increase, PS decreases, DWL created Deadweight Loss
  • Examples: rent control, gas price caps
  • Quantity actually traded = the lesser of Qs or Qd = Qs

⑥ Per-Unit Excise Tax

A per-unit tax shifts the supply curve upward by the tax amount. The price consumers pay rises, the price producers receive falls, and deadweight loss is created.

Key Points

  • Tax wedge = Pc (consumer price) − Pp (producer price)
  • Tax Revenue = Tax × Qt — shared between consumers and producers
  • Consumer tax burden = Pc − Pe  |  Producer tax burden = Pe − Pp
  • More inelastic side bears more of the tax burden
  • DWL Deadweight Loss — triangle between Qt and Qe, between S and S+tax
